Datacenter Signal - Issue 130

Week of June 25 - July 1, 2026

The week in one read

This week the pace of local data-center pushback moved faster than any single utility or developer's response. Loudon County, Tennessee approved a six-month moratorium capping new builds above 50 MW, Seattle's city council enacted a one-year freeze on large facilities, and a grassroots coalition in Ohio closed in on a 413,000-signature threshold for a constitutional ballot measure banning any data center over 25 MW. On the utility side, a reported NextEra-Dominion merger surfaced as the largest regulated-utility combination in US history - a deal whose political weight in Virginia may itself determine whether the data-center build-out in Northern Virginia accelerates or stalls.

Critical developments

1. Loudon County Approves Six-Month Moratorium on New Data Centers

On the evening of Monday June 29, 2026, the Loudon County Commission in eastern Tennessee voted unanimously to impose a six-month moratorium on new data-center permits, according to (3bmedianews) and (wvlt). The moratorium halts county permits, zoning approvals, building approvals, utility extensions, and development agreements, per (wate).

Scope and exemptions. The pause covers facilities projected to draw 50 megawatts or more within their first three years of operation. State-owned facilities and data centers serving telecommunications or broadband networks are exempt. The Loudon County Planning and Codes Director is charged with reviewing and recommending updated zoning and planning rules during the window, per (3bmedianews). Commissioners retain authority to extend the moratorium, per (wvlt).

Why it matters. Loudon County sits inside the Tennessee Valley Authority service territory. A TVA spokesperson noted in coverage that data centers represented roughly 18% of TVA's total industrial power load last year, with that share projected to double by 2030, per (3bmedianews). The cited public concerns in the adopting resolution were noise, power availability, limited local employment generation, and infrastructure burden, per (wvlt).

Implications for infrastructure investors. Loudon County is the first Tennessee Valley county to translate utility-scale AI load growth into a planning-timeout. Comparable local actions in Iron County, Utah ((sltrib)) and Clay County, Florida show a template migrating from Pacific Northwest and Northern Virginia into the Southeast. Investors should expect more 50-100 MW-edge counties to follow and watch the Loudon rezoning output at month three as a leading indicator of whether the break becomes permanent.

What to watch next. Whether Loudon County's June 29 vote is read by neighboring Blount, Knox, and Roane counties as a precedent; whether TVA publishes its industrial-load reforecast; and whether the proposed exceptions (state, telecom, broadband) survive legal challenge. Prior local resistance is documented by the (news-herald).

2. Reported NextEra-Dominion Merger Reshapes Virginia Utility Politics

Reports surfaced this week that NextEra Energy is pursuing an acquisition of Dominion Energy, a combination that would yield an entity with a roughly $250 billion market capitalization - the largest regulated electric utility in the United States, per (eenews). The deal has been framed by Virginia consumer advocates as "more than energy," per (cardinalnews).

Deal mechanics and growth profile. Combined businesses project earnings growth above 9% annually, with a combined rate base growing 11% annually through 2032 and a 130,000 MW pipeline of new large-load demand, per (eenews). Companies have committed to $2.25 billion in customer credits across three states over two years if the deal closes, per (eenews) and (virginiamercury). NextEra executives have additionally floated annual infrastructure investment growth of 11% through 2035, per (cardinalnews). Dividends are also expected to rise, per (eenews).

Regulatory pathway. Approval sits with the Virginia State Corporation Commission, with filings subject to scrutiny by Attorney General Jay Jones, per (cardinalnews) and (wtvr). State Senator Schuyler VanValkenburg acknowledged it will be "really hard to regulate," per (eenews).

Political concentration. Dominion has spent at least $40 million on Virginia elections since 2020, making it the state's largest corporate political spender, per (eenews). Critics argue the deal would "supercharge" that influence. Brennan Gilmore of Clean Virginia characterized the customer credits as a "temporary payment, not a structural reduction in bills," per (cardinalnews). Other criticisms cite NextEra's Florida track record, including a $6.9 billion rate increase in 2025 and past "ghost candidate" scandals, per (cardinalnews).

Why it matters for data centers. Dominion is the utility serving the largest concentration of data centers globally in Loudoun and Prince William counties. A merged entity would inherit Dominion's interest in Northern Virginia Electric Cooperative - NOVEC serves the second-most data centers in Northern Virginia and Dominion was in talks to acquire it as of November, per (virginiamercury). The 130,000 MW large-load pipeline also reads as a back-of-envelope projection of how much interconnection queue the combined utility will be expected to absorb across both states.

What to watch next. The State Corporation Commission's evidentiary hearings and any conditions attached to a merger order; the formal response from Virginia's legislature; whether the $2.25 billion in credits is structured as a one-time bill offset versus a base-rate adjustment; and any movement on the Northern Virginia Electric Cooperative acquisition.

3. Ohio Ballot Initiative Seeks Constitutional Ban on Data Centers Exceeding 25 MW

On July 1, 2026, the deadline arrives for organizers of a proposed Ohio constitutional amendment to submit more than 413,000 valid signatures from at least 44 of the state's 88 counties to qualify for the November ballot, according to (10tv) and (ballotpedia).

Mechanics. The amendment, championed by (conserveohio) and supported by a coalition including the (facebook), would prohibit construction of data centers whose aggregate peak power demand exceeds 25 megawatts per month, per (ballotpedia) and (conserveohio). The campaign launched in March 2026 with a rural Ohio group's petition to the state's Attorney General, per (ohioballotprotection). Signature collection is volunteer-driven.

Coalition and messaging. The effort is led by Southern Ohio residents with public spokespeople including Nikki Gerber and Austin Baurichter, per (10tv). The grassroots framing is a response to a cascade of local-level moratorium activity; Lordstown was the first Ohio town to ban data centers, and county-level pauses have spread across the state in 2026, per the (theoec).

Why it matters. A 25 MW threshold is roughly an order of magnitude below the cutting-edge hyperscale campus; if enacted, it would effectively end Ohio's competitiveness for hyperscale new-builds while preserving room for edge facilities. The vote comes against a backdrop of an active 8-state tally of statewide restrictions under consideration nationally, per (datacenterbans).

What to watch next. Whether the Ohio Attorney General's signature count validates the petition by an August deadline; whether opposition from Ohio utility AEP and hyperscale developers materializes in paid media before the November vote; and any Ohio Supreme Court challenges to ballot language.

Regional roundup

  • Northern Virginia: Loudoun County Board of Supervisors Chair Phyllis Randall schedules a Data Center Town Hall for Monday July 6, 7:00-9:30 p.m. at the County Government Building in Leesburg, with residents receiving seating and Q&A priority ((loudoun)).
  • Atlanta / Georgia: A Fayetteville, GA data center consumed ~29-30 million gallons of water without being metered, surfacing only after residents complained of low pressure, per (politico) and (fortune).
  • Central Ohio: The (facebook) formally endorses Conserve Ohio's 25 MW constitutional ban, broadening the coalition behind the July 1 signature push ((geaugamapleleaf)).
  • Phoenix / Arizona: Arizona Public Service reports commercial load-growth proposals have roughly doubled prior interconnection queue submissions, with about 30 data centers requesting a combined 18 GW and an uncommitted queue near 19 GW, per (utilitydive) and (aps).
  • Utah: Iron County Commission on May 26 adopted a 180-day moratorium on new AI data-center land-use applications, per the (sltrib) and (ironcountyut).
  • Texas: ERCOT's new Batch Zero interconnection process sets a July 10, 2026 deadline for large-load project technical-study submissions and an August 7 deadline for survey packages, against a 438 GW statewide queue, per (utilitydive) and (ercot).
  • Pacific Northwest: Seattle City Council on June 9 unanimously passed a one-year emergency moratorium on new large data centers using more than 20 MVA, paired with a permanent policy framework, per the (council) and (theguardian).
  • Florida: Clay County Board of County Commissioners approved a 12-month moratorium on data-center permits following hearings in early June, per (firstcoastnews) and (wokv), joining Nassau and a pending Leon County pause ((tallahassee)).
  • Northern California: AB 1577 (Bauer-Kahan) was re-referred on June 24 from committee to the Senate Judiciary with a 13-4 vote, advancing monthly energy/water/PUE/WUE reporting for covered data centers, per (legiscan) and (calmatters).
  • Southern California: Monterey Park voters approved Measure NDC with more than 86% support - the first U.S. citizen-initiated permanent data-center ban - per (spectrumlocalnews) and (theguardian).
